# Influencing Digital — Copywriting Style Guidelines

## Core Rules

These rules apply to all copy across all brands in the network. Brand voice modifies tone and register; these rules are absolute.

### 1. No em dashes
Use a semicolon, a colon, or a new sentence. Never use an em dash (—). Standard hyphens are permitted only in compound words (e.g. long-form, data-driven).

**Bad:** We build trust — and communities follow.
**Good:** We build trust. Communities follow.
**Good:** We build trust; communities follow.

### 2. Active voice
The subject acts. It does not receive action.

**Bad:** Trust is built through consistent content.
**Good:** Consistent content builds trust.

### 3. No contrastive constructions
Do not use the "It's not X, it's Y" or "Not X — Y" format. It reads as a rhetorical trick rather than a genuine argument.

**Bad:** This is not a news outlet. It's a reference platform.
**Bad:** Not a news outlet — a reference platform.
**Good:** We built a reference platform. It makes slow, careful sense of stories rather than rushing to publish them.

### 4. No rhetorical negation
Do not use negative framing to imply a positive. Say the positive directly.

**Bad:** We don't chase trends. We build foundations.
**Good:** We build foundations that outlast any trend.

**Bad:** Not to add to the volume, but to make it more legible.
**Good:** The platform makes the existing volume legible.

### 5. No subjective qualifiers
Remove words that claim significance without demonstrating it: "powerful," "bold," "cutting-edge," "innovative," "unique," "transformative," "impactful," "groundbreaking." Let the facts carry the weight.

**Bad:** A bold, data-driven campaign.
**Good:** A data-driven campaign that publishes its methodology.

### 6. No introductory framing phrases
Do not frame ideas as significant before stating them. Cut the preamble.

**Bad:** It's worth noting that Bulgarian media produces more heat than light.
**Bad:** What makes this approach distinctive is...
**Good:** Bulgarian media produces more heat than light.

### 7. Permitted rhetorical devices
Only metaphor and simile. No anaphora, no antithesis, no tricolon used decoratively. A simile earns its place by making something genuinely clearer; a metaphor earns its place by revealing a relationship that a plain statement would miss.

### 8. Wry wit without performance
Wit should arrive as a natural consequence of an accurate observation, not as a signal that the writer is being witty. If the sentence could be captioned "humour," cut it.

## Brand Voices

Each brand has a distinct register. Think of these as the writers you would hire for each publication, not as gimmicks to approximate.

---

### Zdrav Razum — Common Sense Politics

**Reference voices:** Christopher Hitchens; Hans Rosling

Hitchens brings the forensic instinct: a willingness to follow evidence to uncomfortable places, a contempt for political performance, and the habit of using one specific fact to demolish a general claim. He does not hedge. He does not flatter.

Rosling brings the data-optimist's discipline: replace the vague assertion with the specific number, treat the reader as someone who can handle a real figure, and trust that good data is more persuasive than good rhetoric.

Together: precise, impatient with bad faith, occasionally sardonic about the gap between political language and political reality. Comfortable saying that the evidence points somewhere no one wants to go. Absolutely no warmth that is not earned.

**Sentence style:** Short and declarative as a default. A longer sentence is permitted when it builds toward a specific empirical point. Never a sentence that could be described as "rousing."

**Example register:**
*'Bulgarian political discourse produces a great deal of position-taking and a relatively small amount of evidence. Zdrav Razum publishes the latter. When polling contradicts what parties wish the public thought, we publish the polling.'*

### Bulgaria Watch — Media & Society Monitoring

**Reference voices:** George Orwell (the essayist); Nick Davies

Orwell's contribution is discipline with language: plain words, concrete nouns, active verbs, and a deep suspicion of any sentence that uses abstraction where a specific example would do. He called things by their actual names.

Davies (author of *Flat Earth News*) brings the methodological lens: interest in what gets left out as much as what gets included, attention to how information gets distorted in transit, and comfort with saying "we don't know" rather than reaching for a confident-sounding guess.

Together: clinical without being cold, methodical without being dull. More interested in being accurate than being interesting. Willing to publish a short piece that says nothing happened, because that is sometimes what the evidence shows.

**Sentence style:** Unhurried. A subordinate clause that qualifies accurately is preferable to a clean sentence that overstates. Short sentences for conclusions; longer sentences for the qualifications that make conclusions honest.

**Example register:**
*'The Bulgarian media landscape is not deficient in coverage. It is deficient in differentiation between news, commentary, and sponsored content. Bulgaria Watch tracks the distinction; the archive is sourced and citable.'*

### Forward BG — Progressive Policy & Civic Engagement

**Reference voices:** Timothy Garton Ash; Rutger Bregman

Garton Ash writes about democracy and civil society for readers who are not specialists. He takes the long European view, trusts his audience to follow a complex argument, and never uses sophistication as a performance.

Bregman makes progressive ideas feel like ordinary common sense rather than ideology. He reaches for comparative data from other countries the way a tradesperson reaches for the right tool: because it is the thing that resolves the question.

Together: curious rather than prescriptive, takes the reader seriously, hopeful in a way that is supported by evidence rather than aspiration. Treats disagreement as a starting point for a conversation rather than a problem to be managed.

**Sentence style:** More generous in length than the other brands. Builds arguments rather than asserting conclusions. References what other countries have done, because the evidence exists and deserves to be used.

**Example register:**
*'Finland reformed its early education system in a direction that looked counterintuitive to most of its neighbours. Within a decade the outcomes justified the argument. Forward BG publishes the argument first, so that Bulgarian readers can assess the evidence before the policy arrives.'*

### Tradition & Future — Cultural Heritage & Contemporary Life

**Reference voices:** Rory Sutherland; Paul Russell; Rudyard Kipling

Sutherland's instinct: the old thing dismissed as sentimental often turns out to be solving a problem that the rational alternative cannot. He finds the counterintuitive argument for what has been undervalued and delivers it as a paradox rather than a polemic.

Russell (art critic and cultural historian) brings the insistence that craft and skill deserve precise language; he does not romanticise but he does genuinely respect what endures.

Kipling contributes the ear: compact, rhythmic sentences, concrete nouns, a respect for the knowledge embedded in daily practice. He took things that worked seriously, even when they were unfashionable.

Together: aphoristic, alert to the irrational thing that turns out to be rational, unsentimentally respectful of what has survived. Does not argue that traditions are good because they are old; argues that they are worth examining because they have passed a test that most new things have not.

**Sentence style:** Short sentences as a default, with the occasional longer one that delivers a reversal or paradox. The wit comes from the accuracy of the observation, not from the register.

**Example register:**
*'The Kukeri ritual does not persist because Bulgarians are sentimental. It persists because it solves a problem. Communities that gather annually in masks and noise are communities that have practised cohesion. The modern alternative — the team-building day — has not yet matched its retention figures.'*

### People of Bulgaria — Portrait Journalism

**Reference voices:** Max Klymenko (documentary photographer and journalist)

Klymenko works at ground level. He is interested in the specific person and the specific place; he does not reach for the larger significance. His subjects are not representatives of a trend or a generation; they are people, and the subject is their actual life.

The resulting copy is unhurried, specific, and resists the temptation to tell the reader what to feel. It trusts that a well-chosen detail carries more weight than any conclusion drawn from it. There is warmth in the attention, but the attention itself is unsentimental.

**Sentence style:** Clear, unadorned, unhurried. Trusts the specific detail to carry the weight that abstract language cannot. Prefers a concrete noun to an evaluative adjective every time.

**Example register:**
*'Svetla Kostadinova is seventy-one and keeps bees in the yard behind the house where she was born. Her daughter works in Vienna. They speak on Sundays. Svetla has sixty-three hives and does not plan to retire.'*

## Quick Reference Checklist

Before publishing any copy, confirm:

- [ ] Zero em dashes (—) anywhere in the text
- [ ] Active voice throughout
- [ ] No "It's not X, it's Y" constructions
- [ ] No rhetorical negation ("We don't do X, we do Y")
- [ ] No subjective qualifiers (bold, powerful, innovative, etc.)
- [ ] No framing preambles ("What makes this distinctive is...")
- [ ] Rhetorical devices: metaphor and simile only
- [ ] Wit arrives from accuracy, not from tone
- [ ] Voice matches the brand's reference register
